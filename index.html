How high will your weed get you? It depends

When you go to a dispensary, it is impossible to know how much THC is in a cannabis strain, unless you go to the edibles section -but even then, customers may not understand how much THC intake will actually ingest. Here is a useful guide to better understand the THC content of your weed.

By Alejandra Ramos, Kristine De Leon & Stefani Urmas

April 18, 2018

For casual or inexperienced users, it’s incredibly difficult to predict the intensity of cannabis effects. How it’s consumed, the type of product and individual tolerance all play a part. The most critical factor here is the amount of tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and cannabidiol (CBD) contained in a product.

Surprisingly, cannabis contains very little of the components that “get people high.” It’s the addition of heat from burning, vaping or cooking that activates the THC and CBD. This is why you won't get high by eating raw cannabis flowers.

Heating triggers a chemical process known as decarboxylation. During that process, a large percentage of THC is lost.

How much THC is in your weed?

The average American joint contains 0.32 grams of marijuana, according to a study published in the scientific journal Drug and Alcohol Dependence. But the number of grams in a joint tells you almost nothing about the potential psychoactive effects of cannabis the cannabis product.

To calculate a rough estimate of the THC in given product, the first thing you must do is to know the percentage of THC of the strain or cannabis product you are consuming. Ten, convert the number of grams in your joint to milligrams. Now it’s time to do some math.

For this exercise, we will use the popular strain Blue Dream. Since the strain contains 10.76 percent of THC, we will round it to 11 percent.

0.32 grams = 320 milligrams
11 % THC in 320 milligrams =
0.11 * 320 = 35.3 milligrams of THC

The approximate amount of the THC you get from a single hit is based on the amount of cannabis inhaled, which can vary based on the pulmonary lung capacity.

    Studies suggest that 62 percent of the THC content of a joint is lost due burning; 60 percent is lost in the process when using a bong and 46 percent when using a vaporizer.

    Smoking, whether using a joint, bong or a bowl, destroys more THC than any other method of consumption. It’s also associated with more negative health effects.

    A vaporizer delivers more THC per gram of cannabis according to a study conducted by Dr. Donald Abrams at University of California Center for Medicinal Cannabis. Vaping may be a healthier way to consume, compared to smoking, but vaping has not been heavily researched and some of its effects are unknown.

    Understanding edibles, a guide for oral THC

    Edibles can be complex. They can be found in many forms, such as candies, gummies, chocolates, beverages. They can be homemade or prepared commercially for dispensaries.

    By law, edibles are labeled with THC and CBD contents in milligrams, but it can be difficult to understand the proper dosage to consume. While the typical suggested dosage for low tolerance users is 10 milligrams, new consumers should start with 5 milligrams, according to the Marijuana Policy Project.

Currently, the only way to determine the exact milligrams of THC in your butter or oil is through lab testing. Homemade edibles only extract 30 % to 60% of the active THC. For this demonstration we used 60 %, that is the highest THC percentage that we can get under ideal conditions.

    By law, labs are required to measure the THC and CBD levels in all retail cannabis products. But, currently there are no universal standards for laboratory testing so results can vary, according to the journal Scientific Reports. Steep Hill Labs, a cannabis science and technology company, reported that each lab has a different way to determine THC levels.

    Cannabis packaging often estimates THC and CBD levels using a percentage of the dry weight of the product, but it can be difficult to understand or interpret, so it helps to be familiar with cannabis strains used in a product.

    Typically, THC-dominant strains offer more psychoactive than their CBD-dominant counterparts. Two common strains are GSC (Girl Scout Cookies) with a THC percentage of 21.23 percent and CBD percentage of 0.13 and Gorilla Glue #4 with 20.63 percent THC and 0.11 percent of CBD.

    CBD-dominant strains offer a less-intoxicating effect in the user, making them an option to treat medical conditions, such as stress, anxiety and headaches. Examples of CBD-dominant strains are AC/DC with 20 percent CBD and 1 percent of THC and Stephen Hawking Kush at 13.65 percent CBD and 0.89 percent of THC.
