A simple guide to pot, THC and how much you ingest

When you go to a dispensary, it is impossible to know how much THC is in a cannabis strain, unless you go to the edibles section -but even then, customers may not understand how much THC intake will actually ingest. Here is a useful guide to better understand the THC content of your weed.

By Alejandra Ramos, Kristine De Leon & Stefani Urmas

April 18, 2018

It is impossible to know exactly how much THC is in a cannabis product and how it will affect someone. The way it’s consumed, the type of product and individual tolerance all play a part.

Cannabis flower products aren’t usually tested in a lab so potency is estimated by dry weight. And many labs use different standards to measure compounds in edibles.

The most critical factor is the amount of tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and cannabidiol (CBD) contained in a product.

Surprisingly, cannabis contains very little of the components that “get people high.” It’s the addition of heat from burning, vaping or cooking that activates the THC and CBD. This is why you won't get high by eating raw cannabis flowers. “Eating raw cannabis flowers will only serve you as an anti-inflammatory,” said Jeffrey Raber, co-founder and president of The Werc Shop, a lab-testing facility based in Los Angeles.

Heating triggers a chemical process known as decarboxylation. During that process, a large percentage of THC is lost.

Smoking, whether using a joint, bong or a bowl, degrades more THC than any other method of consumption. It’s also associated with more negative health effects.

A vaporizer delivers more THC per gram of cannabis, according to a study conducted by Dr. Donald Abrams at University of California Center for Medicinal Cannabis. Vaping may be a healthier way to consume, compared to smoking, but vaping has not been heavily researched and some of its effects are unknown.

    Smoking vs. eating

    There is a big difference between inhaling and eating cannabis because the THC in edibles is metabolized by the liver. It can take from 30 minutes to 2 hours to start feeling the effects, which will be stronger and last longer when compared to smoking or vaping.

    When smoking or using a vaporizer, psychotropics effects start within seconds. Eaten cannabis sets in after 30 to 90 minutes and its maximum is reached after 2 to 3 hours. Edibles have slow build and long effect.

    Edibles can be found in many forms, such as candies, gummies, chocolates, beverages. They can be homemade or prepared commercially for dispensaries.

    By law, edibles are labeled with THC and CBD contents in milligrams, but it can be difficult to understand what’s an appropriate amount to consume. While the typical suggested dosage for low tolerance users is 10 milligrams, new consumers should start with 5 milligrams, according to the Marijuana Policy Project.

    Baking with cannabis can be much more dangerous than eating consumer edibles because home cooks are generally less consistent in the amount used and serving sizes.
